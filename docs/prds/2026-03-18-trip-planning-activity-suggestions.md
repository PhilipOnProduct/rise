# Rise — Trip Planning PRD

---

## Overview

This PRD covers two related but distinct features surfaced in product discussion: **activities-first onboarding sequencing** and **map-based trip visualization**. They share a strategic direction — moving Rise from a recommendation engine toward a trip planning tool — but have different complexity profiles, different timelines, and must be treated as separate experiments.

Neither ships before we validate the current restaurant AI preview. This document defines what we're building, in what order, and why.

---

## Problem Statement

The current onboarding flow collects destination, dates, and hotel, then surfaces AI restaurant recommendations. The gap: **we never help the user form a mental picture of their trip before asking them to make supporting decisions.** Restaurants are logistics. Activities are the reason people travel.

The hypothesis is that the restaurant recommendation lands flat because the user has no activity frame yet — they don't know what neighborhood they'll be in, what kind of day they're having, or what the trip even feels like. We're asking them to decide where to eat before they've decided what they're doing.

A secondary problem: Rise currently has no concept of a structured itinerary. A trip is destination + dates + hotel. Nothing more. That data shape limits every planning feature we might want to build — including the map visualization — and the limitation is invisible until you try to build on top of it.

---

## User Need

Users plan trips in a specific mental order: destination → anchor experiences → logistics. Right now Rise interrupts that sequence by jumping to logistics (restaurants) before anchor experiences (activities) are established.

The core unmet need is: **help me figure out what I'm actually doing on this trip, in a sequence that matches how I naturally think about travel.**

A secondary need, identified as emotionally compelling but not yet validated for retention: **let me see my trip as a whole, not as a list of disconnected recommendations.**

---

## Proposed Solution

Three sequential phases. Each phase gates the next.

**Phase 1 — Ship and measure (now)**
Ship the restaurant AI preview at onboarding step 3 as currently designed. Run five observed sessions. Ask one falsifiable question per session: does the restaurant recommendation feel useful, or does it feel like a demo?

**Phase 2 — Activities-first sequencing (conditional on Phase 1 findings)**
If sessions show users are lost, disengaged, or compensating (opening Google Maps, referencing activities unprompted), swap the restaurant AI preview for an activity suggestion moment. This is a prompt change and a UI component swap on existing streaming infrastructure — Luca estimates an afternoon's work. The onboarding wizard already has a step 4 slot for activities in the schema.

This phase does not require a structured itinerary data model. It's still a streaming AI text output — richer in content, identical in architecture.

**Phase 3 — Map-based itinerary visualization (post-MVP, separate experiment)**
A timeline-map view where selecting a day highlights that day's activities on the map. This is the most emotionally differentiating idea in the discussion — the kind of moment that makes someone screenshot the app and send it to their travel partner.

It is also the most expensive to build and cannot ship meaningfully until we have structured itinerary data. Phase 3 begins with a schema decision, not a design decision.

---

## User Stories

**Activities sequencing**

- As a user beginning trip planning, I want to see suggested activities for my destination before I'm asked about restaurants, so that my dining decisions have context.
- As a user, I want the AI to suggest activities that fit my trip dates and duration, so I can start forming a realistic picture of what I'll do each day.
- As a user, I want to be able to skip activity suggestions and continue onboarding, so the flow doesn't feel mandatory or blocking.

**Map visualization**

- As a user who has built a trip itinerary, I want to see my activities plotted on a map, so I can understand the geography of my plans at a glance.
- As a user reviewing my itinerary, I want to select a specific day and see only that day's activities highlighted on the map, so I can think through daily logistics without visual noise.
- As a user, I want the map view to update in real time as I add or modify activities, so the visualization stays accurate without manual refresh.

---

## Success Metrics

**Phase 1 — Restaurant validation**

- Primary: Qualitative signal from 5 sessions — does the recommendation moment read as useful or as a demo?
- Watch for: compensatory behavior (Google Maps opened in another tab), spontaneous activity references during the restaurant step, body language at the recommendation moment (lean in vs. sit back)
- Decision threshold: If 3 of 5 users show confusion or compensatory behavior at the restaurant step, proceed to Phase 2

**Phase 2 — Activities sequencing**

- Onboarding completion rate (step 3 → step 5): target parity with or improvement over restaurant baseline
- Time spent on activity suggestion step: proxy for engagement; under 15 seconds suggests the moment isn't landing
- Qualitative: do users reference their activity suggestions later in the session?

**Phase 3 — Map visualization**

- Day-selection interactions per session: target at least 2 per active planning session
- Return visits to map view within 48 hours of itinerary creation: measures whether the map drives retention or is a one-time delight
- Screenshot or share events: proxy for the emotional resonance hypothesis
- Do not ship Phase 3 without a baseline retention metric from Phase 2 to compare against

## Technical Considerations

**Activities sequencing (low complexity)**
No new infrastructure required. Swap the system prompt, update the UI component to reflect activity framing. The onboarding wizard step 4 slot already exists in the schema. Estimate: half a day.

The output is still streamed markdown. Activities-first is a content and sequencing decision, not an architectural one.

**Itinerary data model (prerequisite for Phase 3)**
Before any map work begins, the team needs to align on what a structured itinerary looks like in Postgres. Current AI output is freeform markdown — nothing is pinnable to a map coordinate. Moving to Phase 3 requires:

- A day-activity relationship model in Postgres (activities with time slots, day assignments, and location data)
- Structured JSON AI responses instead of streamed markdown, with a parsing layer to write activity rows to the database
- Reliable geocoding for activity locations

This is a half-day schema conversation that determines whether Phase 3 is a two-week build or a two-month one. It should happen before any Phase 3 design work, not after.

**Map visualization (high complexity)**
Requires a mapping integration (Mapbox or Google Maps), a geocoded activity dataset, and a day-filtering interaction layer on top of the map. The day-highlighting interaction is the most visually appealing part of the idea and the last thing that can be built — it depends on everything below it being solid.

Do not begin Phase 3 UI design until the itinerary schema is finalized and at least one structured AI response is successfully parsed into Postgres rows.

## Risks & Open Questions

**Risk: Shipping activities and the map together**
If both ship in the same release, we will not know which change drove any behavior. These must be separate experiments with separate measurement moments. Bundling them is how you spend two months building and learn nothing. This is the highest-priority risk in this document.

**Risk: Map engagement follows the research pattern, not the concept-test pattern**
Users consistently rate map visualizations highly in concept tests and consistently underuse them in live products. TripIt and Google Trips both had this feature; engagement data was always weaker than enthusiasm in research. We should build the map when we have evidence that our users are returning to manage their trip — not as an onboarding hook designed to impress during demos.

**Risk: Scope creep from Phase 3 anchoring**
If the team starts designing toward the map now, small architectural choices will begin accommodating a feature that's months away. This is how scope creep starts. The itinerary schema conversation is the right place to surface and contain this risk — not the design phase.

**Open question: Is Rise a recommendation engine or a trip planning tool?**
This is the actual strategic decision underneath everything in this PRD. A recommendation engine is destination + dates + hotel → AI suggestions. A trip planning tool requires a structured itinerary as a first-class data object with a different schema, a different user mental model, and a different competitive position. Both are valid. They lead to different roadmaps. This question should be answered explicitly after Phase 1 sessions, before Phase 2 begins — not deferred indefinitely.

**Open question: What does the AI need to output for Phase 3?** Streamed markdown is fine for Phase 2. Phase 3 requires structured JSON with location data for every activity so it can be geocoded and pinned. The prompt design for Phase 3 is different enough that it should be designed separately. Don't try to make one prompt do both jobs.
